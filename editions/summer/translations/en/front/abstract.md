# Abstract

**Human in the Loop Machine Learning: Misunderstood**

The dominant narrative of machine learning positions the human as a temporary participant — present during data collection and model training, then progressively removed as automation improves. This handbook argues that this narrative is wrong, has always been wrong, and that understanding *why* it is wrong is one of the most practically useful things a machine learning practitioner can do.

Human-in-the-Loop (HITL) Machine Learning is the discipline of making human involvement in learning systems deliberate, structured, and ongoing. Rather than treating human feedback as a cost to be minimized, HITL treats it as a signal to be understood: where it comes from, what it encodes, how to collect it efficiently, and how to incorporate it faithfully into models that behave as their designers and users actually intend.

This handbook provides a comprehensive treatment of the field across six parts. Part I establishes foundations and a taxonomy of human–machine interaction. Part II covers the classical techniques: annotation and labeling, active learning, and interactive machine learning. Part III addresses the modern paradigms of alignment: reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF), learning from demonstrations, and preference learning. Part IV applies these techniques to natural language processing, computer vision, and healthcare. Part V covers the systems and infrastructure of HITL at scale: annotation platforms, crowdsourcing quality control, and evaluation methodology. Part VI examines the ethics and future of the field.

Each chapter includes formal definitions, mathematical treatment where appropriate, and self-contained runnable code examples in Python.

The handbook is intended for practitioners who build ML systems, researchers who study them, students who are learning about them, and anyone who has ever wondered who labeled the data that trained the model that made the decision that affected their life.

The answer, in all cases, is: a human. Often underpaid. Rarely credited. Always in the loop.
