# Acknowledgments

Every book about human-in-the-loop machine learning should begin with the humans in its own loop.

**The annotators.** This handbook is, in part, about you — about your labor, your judgment, and the fact that your name appears in no dataset card, paper acknowledgment, or press release about the models your work made possible. The field is beginning to reckon with this. It has a long way to go.

**The open-source contributors** whose libraries are used in every code example: the NumPy community (since 2006), the scikit-learn contributors (since 2007), the PyTorch team, the Hugging Face community, and the countless maintainers who fix bugs on weekends without compensation or recognition. Scientific computing is built on this invisible labor.

**The researchers** who published their work openly — in arXiv preprints, open-access venues, and GitHub repositories — making it possible for practitioners to learn without institutional access. And the researchers who reviewed papers carefully and honestly, a form of expert annotation that academia has somehow convinced itself is not labor.

**The teachers** who made knowledge accessible: the bloggers, the tutorial writers, the Stack Overflow answerers, the conference presenters who posted their slides. Learning does not happen in isolation, and it does not happen without people willing to explain things to strangers.

**The critics.** The researchers who pointed out that datasets encode biases, that annotator demographics shape model behavior, that the humans in the loop are often the most vulnerable participants in the system. This field is better for your insistence on asking uncomfortable questions.
