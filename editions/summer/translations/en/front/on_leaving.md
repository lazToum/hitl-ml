# On Leaving

At some point I was enrolled in a PhD program. The research was on machine learning. The institution was real. The intent was genuine.

And then I stopped.

Not because the subject stopped mattering — it matters more now than when I started. Not because the work was too hard — this handbook is harder, in its own way, than a thesis would have been. But because somewhere between the literature review and the third revision of a paper that would be read by eleven people, I lost the thread of why any of it was worth doing.

The PhD system is a loop. You enter it as a human with questions. The loop teaches you to convert those questions into papers, and papers into citations, and citations into the appearance of progress. At no point does the loop ask whether the output is useful to anyone outside the loop. That question is, in the technical sense of the term, out of scope.

I am not the first person to notice this. I will not be the last. This is not a criticism — it is a description. The loop produces real things: rigorous methods, accumulated knowledge, trained researchers. I do not regret the time I spent inside it. But I did eventually notice that I was in it.

Leaving was not a dramatic decision. It was a slow recognition that the thing I actually wanted to build did not require permission from a committee. That the people I wanted to reach — practitioners, students, engineers making real decisions about real systems — were not waiting for a formally validated thesis. That the most honest thing I could do with what I knew was to write it down in a form someone could actually use.

So I did.

This handbook is not a PhD thesis. It has no institutional affiliation, no funding acknowledgment, no ethics board approval, no examination. It will not count toward any metric that academia uses to measure the production of knowledge.

It is also — and I say this without false modesty, because false modesty would be its own kind of dishonesty — more complete, more current, more readable, and more useful than the thesis would have been.

I am a human in the loop. I was in the loop of a PhD program, and I stepped out of it. I am now in the loop of writing about loops. You are in the loop of reading about it.

None of us chose to be in these loops entirely freely. But we can choose, sometimes, to be honest about which ones we are in, and why, and whether they are producing anything worth producing.

This book is my answer to those questions.

It is not a resignation letter. Resignation implies a job, and a job implies that someone else decided the terms. This is something else: a record of what I chose to do with the time, and a quiet argument that the choice was the right one.

You can be the judge of that. You are, after all, a human in the loop.
