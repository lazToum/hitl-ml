## Automation Bias: Core Papers

Skitka, L. J., Mosier, K. L., & Burdick, M. (1999). Does automation bias decision-making? *International Journal of Human-Computer Studies, 51*(5), 991–1006.
> The foundational experimental paper demonstrating that automation bias persists even under explicit independence instructions.

Parasuraman, R., & Riley, V. (1997). Humans and automation: Use, misuse, disuse, abuse. *Human Factors, 39*(2), 230–253.
> The definitive taxonomy of human-automation interaction; distinguishes use, misuse (automation bias), disuse (algorithm aversion), and abuse.

Cummings, M. L. (2004). Automation bias in intelligent time critical decision support systems. In *AIAA 1st Intelligent Systems Technical Conference.* American Institute of Aeronautics and Astronautics.

Goddard, K., Roudsari, A., & Wyatt, J. C. (2012). Automation bias: A systematic review of frequency, effect mediators, and mitigators. *Journal of the American Medical Informatics Association, 19*(1), 121–127.
> Best available systematic review; covers medical, aviation, and process control domains.

## Automation Complacency

Parasuraman, R., Molloy, R., & Singh, I. L. (1993). Performance consequences of automation-induced complacency. *International Journal of Aviation Psychology, 3*(1), 1–23.

Bainbridge, L. (1983). Ironies of automation. *Automatica, 19*(6), 775–779.
> The original "ironies of automation" paper; among the most-cited papers in human factors. Required reading for anyone designing HITL systems.

Endsley, M. R. (1995). Toward a theory of situation awareness in dynamic systems. *Human Factors, 37*(1), 32–64.
> Introduces and formalizes the concept of situation awareness, directly relevant to understanding AF447 and similar handoff failures.

Endsley, M. R. (2017). From here to autonomy: Lessons learned from human–automation research. *Human Factors, 59*(1), 5–27.
> Update on 20 years of research; addresses modern AI systems specifically.

## Overtrust and Undertrust (Algorithm Aversion)

Dietvorst, B. J., Logg, J. M., & Logg, J. M. (2015). Algorithm aversion: People erroneously avoid algorithms after seeing them err. *Journal of Experimental Psychology: General, 144*(1), 114–126.
> Key study showing that seeing an algorithm err once causes lasting over-avoidance, even when the algorithm still outperforms human judgment.

Logg, J. M., Minson, J. A., & Moore, D. A. (2019). Algorithm appreciation: People prefer algorithmic to human judgment. *Organizational Behavior and Human Decision Processes, 151*, 90–103.
> The opposite finding: in some contexts, algorithm trust exceeds human trust. Useful for illustrating that the direction of trust miscalibration depends on domain and context.

Dietvorst, B. J., & Bharti, S. (2020). People reject algorithms in uncertain decision domains because they have diminishing sensitivity to forecasting error. *Psychological Science, 31*(10), 1302–1314.

## Signal Detection Theory

Green, D. M., & Swets, J. A. (1966). *Signal Detection Theory and Psychophysics*. Wiley.
> The foundational text. Mathematical treatment; not required for conceptual understanding.

Macmillan, N. A., & Creelman, C. D. (2005). *Detection Theory: A User's Guide* (2nd ed.). Lawrence Erlbaum.
> More accessible than Green & Swets; includes practical computation guidance.

Swets, J. A., Dawes, R. M., & Monahan, J. (2000). Psychological science can improve diagnostic decisions. *Psychological Science in the Public Interest, 1*(1), 1–26.
> Applied SDT for medical and legal decision making; excellent gateway for social science audiences.

## Human Performance, Fatigue, and Vigilance

Danziger, S., Levav, J., & Avnaim-Pesso, L. (2011). Extraneous factors in judicial decisions. *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences, 108*(17), 6889–6892.
> The Israeli parole board study; directionally replicated even as methodological debates continue.

Mackworth, N. H. (1948). The breakdown of vigilance during prolonged visual search. *Quarterly Journal of Experimental Psychology, 1*(1), 6–21.
> The foundational vigilance study; still the baseline reference for time-on-task performance decay.

Linder, J. A., Doctor, J. N., Friedberg, M. W., Nieva, H. R., Birks, C., Meeker, D., & Fox, C. R. (2014). Time of day and the decision to prescribe antibiotics. *JAMA Internal Medicine, 174*(12), 2029–2031.
> Real-world evidence of time-of-day effects on clinical decision making.

## Expertise and Human-AI Interaction

Chi, M. T. H., Glaser, R., & Rees, E. (1982). Expertise in problem solving. In R. Sternberg (Ed.), *Advances in the Psychology of Human Intelligence* (Vol. 1). Lawrence Erlbaum.

Kahneman, D. (2011). *Thinking, Fast and Slow*. Farrar, Straus and Giroux.
> Part II on cognitive biases highly relevant to expertise effects; accessible for general audience.

Dawes, R. M., Faust, D., & Meehl, P. E. (1989). Clinical versus actuarial judgment. *Science, 243*(4899), 1668–1674.
> The classic paper showing that simple actuarial models outperform clinical expertise on many prediction tasks — directly relevant to when expert HITL reviewers should and should not override AI.
