# Chapter 18 Teacher's Guide: Your Role in This Future

*Pedagogical guide for instructors — the closing chapter*

---

## Instructor Overview

Chapter 18 is the closing chapter, and it should feel different from the others. The goal is not to introduce new content but to complete an arc: from the opening question "are you still watching?" to the closing invitation "your job is not to approve what the machine decided — your job is to be present."

**Key pedagogical goals:**
1. Distinguish the human's enduring role from AI-limitation arguments and ground it in accountability, lived experience, and moral responsibility
2. Concretize new HITL roles — moving from abstract claims to specific, growing job categories
3. Connect individual skills development to civic participation in AI governance
4. End the course with a call to action, not a summary

**Pedagogical note:** This chapter works best when students bring synthesis from the whole course. Consider assigning a brief pre-class reflection: "What has most changed in your thinking about human-AI collaboration?"

---

## Learning Objectives

1. Articulate at least three reasons the human role is not contingent on AI limitations
2. Identify at least four specific HITL roles and describe the skills each requires
3. Distinguish skills most valuable in human-AI collaboration from generic "skills AI can't replicate"
4. Explain civic participation in AI governance with a concrete example
5. Describe what "genuine presence" means in HITL systems vs. "performance of oversight"

## Discussion Questions

### Opening Synthesis (15 minutes)

**Q0:** Looking back over the full arc of the book: what is the single most important thing you now understand about human-AI collaboration that you didn't before? What has changed in how you interact daily with AI systems?

### On What AI Cannot Replace

**Q1:** The chapter argues the human role is permanent not because AI *can't* do certain things yet, but because accountability, value judgment, and lived experience are fundamentally human functions. What's the difference between these two arguments? Why does it matter?

**Q2:** The Air Canada case: the company, not the chatbot, was responsible for the chatbot's error. What are the limits of a legal/regulatory framework built around AI accountability? What would need to change for AI systems to be accountable without a human intermediary?

**Q3:** "A model trained on data about a community is not the same as a member of that community exercising judgment about it." What is the epistemological difference? Can a model ever acquire lived experience?

**Q4:** "When you review an AI's recommendation and confirm or override it, you are taking responsibility for the outcome." Many people don't experience their role this way. What organizational and design conditions would cause someone to genuinely internalize this responsibility?

### On New Roles and Skills

**Q5:** Which of the six HITL roles (annotation specialist, auditor/fairness analyst, oversight coordinator, red teamer, conversational AI evaluator, policy specialist) requires the most genuinely hybrid skill set? Which could be performed by someone with only domain expertise? Only technical expertise?

**Q6:** "Domain expertise combined with AI literacy is dramatically more valuable than domain expertise alone." What does AI literacy mean specifically for: a doctor? A lawyer? A journalist? A social worker?

**Q7:** Adversarial thinking — understanding how AI systems fail, what their blind spots are, how they can be gamed — is described as increasingly valuable. Is this skill teachable? What would a course in it look like?

### On Civic Participation

**Q8:** You are already in the loop. The aggregate of billions of micro-interactions constitutes the human signal AI systems rely on — mostly without your knowledge or consent. Is this a problem? What would meaningful consent look like at scale?

**Q9:** The most important questions in AI governance are not primarily technical. For each: (a) recidivism algorithm in sentencing, (b) facial recognition in public spaces, (c) who bears cost of AI errors — what technical knowledge is needed? What non-technical knowledge is equally important?

**Q10:** Most AI regulatory comment periods attract submissions predominantly from tech companies and professional advocates. What barriers prevent individuals from participating? Which are practical (time, awareness) and which are structural (process design)?

### The Closing Invitation

**Q11:** The flight attendant story: genuine presence elicits genuine presence. What design choices make it more or less likely a human reviewer will be genuinely present rather than going through the motions?

**Q12:** Final synthesis: The book began with Netflix asking "are you still watching?" and ends with the invitation to be present. What is the connection? What does the book's answer look like across all five dimensions of the framework?

## Classroom Activities

### Activity 1: Skills Gap Analysis (20 minutes)

Students identify a domain they know well and a specific AI application in that domain.

**Tasks:**
1. Describe the AI application: what does it do, what human work does it affect?
2. What domain expertise would someone need to critically evaluate its outputs?
3. What AI literacy would they additionally need?
4. Describe a specific failure that would be invisible to someone with only domain OR only technical expertise — but visible to someone with both.

### Activity 2: Civic Participation Audit (25 minutes)

Students find one current AI regulatory process (NIST AI RMF, EU AI Act implementation, FTC AI guidelines, municipal facial recognition policy).

**Tasks:**
1. Read the draft document's key provisions
2. Identify AI literacy concepts from this course that are relevant
3. Draft three sentences of substantive public comment (not just "I support/oppose")
4. What additional information would make your comment stronger?

### Activity 3: Theater vs. Presence Audit (15 minutes)

Students classify five recent digital interactions with AI systems as:
- **Theater:** Nominal human interaction — clicked through without meaningful engagement
- **Genuine:** Actually exercised judgment

For each theater interaction: what would it take to engage genuinely? For each genuine one: what made you engage — design, stakes, framing?

## Common Misconceptions

**Misconception 1:** "The chapter says AI won't replace jobs."

*Correction:* The chapter is more careful. It says specific functions of accountability, value judgment, and lived experience create a permanent human role — not that AI won't displace routine labor. The argument is about the *character* of the enduring role, not its scale.

**Misconception 2:** "Civic participation requires technical expertise."

*Correction:* The chapter's central claim is the opposite. The most important questions are about values, power, and accountability — not resolved by technical expertise. Someone who has read this book has enough literacy to participate meaningfully in most public governance processes.

---

## Closing Notes

This is the last class session. Consider ending differently:
- Ask each student to name one AI system they'll interact with this week and describe what "genuine presence" would mean for that interaction
- Read the closing paragraph aloud and ask for a moment of reflection
- Exchange reflection letters and respond to a classmate's

The closing line — "Welcome to the loop" — should feel like an opening, not a conclusion.
