\subsubsection*{Q0: Course Synthesis Reflection}

This question does not have a correct answer. Strong responses will identify a specific argument (not a general theme) and explain why it changed their thinking. Common strong answers:

\begin{description}
    \item[Calibration as a HITL property, not just a model property:] Students who arrive thinking calibration is a technical model characteristic often leave understanding it as the foundation of the trust relationship between human and AI system.
    \item[The fairness impossibility:] Many students arrive assuming fairness is achievable with enough effort. Understanding that the impossibility is structural---not a consequence of insufficient technology or bad intentions---is genuinely new for most students.
    \item[Annotation encodes choices:] Students who thought of training data as neutral ``facts'' often find the annotation chapter most transformative.
    \item[Compliance theater:] Students often recognize in retrospect that most human oversight they have observed (in workplaces, in their own digital interactions) has been theatrical.
\end{description}

\subsubsection*{Q1: AI-limitation argument vs.\ permanent role argument}

\textbf{The AI-limitation argument:} ``Humans are irreplaceable because AI systems can't yet do X (reason, create, empathize, etc.).'' This is a contingent, empirical claim. As AI capabilities advance, each specific limitation becomes a moving target. The argument provides no durable basis for human participation---only a temporary one.

\textbf{The permanent role argument:} ``The human role is permanent because accountability, value judgment, and lived experience are functions that require a human agent---not because of technical limitations, but because of what these functions mean in a social and moral context.'' This is a structural claim about what accountability and moral responsibility are, not what AI systems can currently do.

The distinction matters because it affects how we respond to AI capability advances. Under the limitation argument, we should be worried about each new AI capability---it narrows the human role. Under the permanent role argument, AI capability advances do not threaten the human role; they change its character (the cases routed to human judgment become harder, more consequential, more genuinely requiring of human presence).

\subsubsection*{Q2: Limits of AI accountability frameworks}

The Air Canada case establishes that corporations are accountable for AI systems they deploy. This is a workable framework for the near term, but it has limits:

\textbf{Attribution difficulty:} As AI systems become more complex and decisions emerge from systems built, trained, and deployed by multiple parties, attributing a specific harm to a specific actor becomes more difficult. The corporation deploying the system, the vendor who built it, and the data provider who trained it may all bear partial responsibility.

\textbf{Diffuse harm:} Many AI harms are diffuse---a credit scoring bias affects thousands of people by a small amount, not one person by a large amount. Traditional accountability frameworks are built around discrete, causally attributable harms.

\textbf{Speed of deployment:} AI systems are deployed, updated, and retired faster than legal accountability can track. By the time a harm is identified and litigated, the specific model may no longer exist.

For AI systems to be accountable without a human intermediary: the system would need a legal personhood equivalent to corporations (with assets and liability), an ability to give explanations that courts can evaluate, and a mechanism for being sanctioned (what does it mean to sanction an algorithm?). None of these is straightforward. The chapter's claim that accountability requires a human agent reflects this structural difficulty.

\subsubsection*{Q3: Model knowledge vs.\ community member knowledge}

A model trained on data about a community has:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Statistical regularities in the recorded behavior of community members
    \item Correlations between observable characteristics and outcomes that were recorded
    \item Whatever was selected for observation and recording by whoever designed the data collection
\end{itemize}

A community member exercising judgment has:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Situated knowledge of the gap between how community members present to external observers and how they actually live
    \item Understanding of historical context not encoded in any dataset
    \item Awareness of what is not represented in the data---the things that weren't measured, the voices that weren't heard
    \item Accountability to the community---they will face the social consequences of their judgment
\end{itemize}

Could a model acquire lived experience? Current architectures cannot experience anything---they process patterns in training data. Even a physically embodied AI that interacted with the world would face a question about whether interaction is sufficient for experience in the morally relevant sense. The question is not currently settled in philosophy of mind; it is a live research question in AI alignment.

\subsubsection*{Q4: Conditions for genuine responsibility internalization}

Design and organizational conditions that foster genuine responsibility internalization:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Feedback on outcomes:} Reviewers who receive information about what happened to cases they reviewed---whether the decision turned out to be correct---develop a genuine sense of their role in outcomes.
    \item \textbf{Meaningful authority to override:} Reviewers who have real authority to override the algorithm (and are not penalized for doing so) experience their judgment as consequential.
    \item \textbf{Appropriate pacing:} Review tasks that don't require decisions faster than human judgment can operate give reviewers the cognitive space to actually engage.
    \item \textbf{Accountability for systematic errors:} Organizations that track reviewer performance---not just throughput, but quality---create incentives for genuine engagement.
    \item \textbf{Case diversity:} Systems that route genuinely varied cases preserve the reviewer's sense of decision-making; systems that route homogeneous easy cases create a checking function that feels routine.
\end{itemize}

The design implication: genuine presence requires that the system give the reviewer something real to do, real authority to act on it, and real feedback about whether they acted well.

\subsubsection*{Q6: Domain-specific AI literacy}

\begin{description}
    \item[Doctor:] Understanding what the model is actually measuring (not diagnosis, but pattern-matching to training data); awareness of distribution shift (a model trained on one hospital may perform differently at another); ability to detect confident-but-wrong outputs (high confidence $\neq$ correctness); awareness of what was not represented in training data.

    \item[Lawyer:] Understanding that AI legal research tools extrapolate from training data and can hallucinate citations; awareness of jurisdictional differences the model may not have represented; ability to verify AI-generated case summaries against original sources; awareness of recency limitations (training cutoffs).

    \item[Journalist:] Understanding that AI-generated factual claims require verification from primary sources; awareness of training data bias in what information is well-represented; ability to detect confidently stated errors; understanding of what AI-generated content signals about the state of public information on a topic.

    \item[Social worker:] Understanding that risk assessment tools encode historical patterns in social services data that may reflect past discriminatory interventions; awareness that risk scores are population-level predictions applied to individual cases; ability to identify when a client's situation is genuinely outside the model's training distribution.
\end{description}

\subsubsection*{Q7: Is adversarial thinking teachable?}

Adversarial thinking is partly a disposition (the willingness to look for failure modes rather than confirming correct operation) and partly a skill (the knowledge of where AI systems typically fail). Both components are teachable.

\textbf{What would a course look like:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Case studies of real AI failures, with analysis of how each failure could have been anticipated
    \item Practical red teaming exercises: given a deployed system and its documentation, find three failure modes
    \item Understanding of common failure mode categories: distribution shift, proxy variables, feedback loops, confident errors, minority subgroup underperformance
    \item Domain-specific failure mode taxonomies: medical AI fails differently than language AI fails differently than computer vision AI fails
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Students' existing domain knowledge:} Any domain in which the student has expertise is a foundation for adversarial thinking about AI systems in that domain. A student who knows legal citation formats can immediately identify when a legal AI has hallucinated a case. A student who knows chemistry can immediately identify when a chemistry AI has generated a plausible-sounding but impossible molecule.

\subsubsection*{Q8: Testing vs.\ red teaming}

\textbf{Testing:} ``Does the system do what it was designed to do in the cases we designed it for?'' Testing is about confirmation---verifying that specified functionality works as intended.

\textbf{Red teaming:} ``How can this system fail, and how would an adversary (or an unexpected deployment context) cause it to fail?'' Red teaming is about discovery---finding failure modes that weren't anticipated in the design.

The mindset shift required: from ``user who wants the system to work'' to ``adversary who wants to find where it doesn't.'' This requires:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Deliberately looking for edge cases rather than typical cases
    \item Generating inputs that violate design assumptions rather than testing within them
    \item Asking ``what would I do if I wanted this system to fail?'' and following the answer
    \item Accepting that finding a failure mode is success, not a criticism of the system
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection*{Q9: Micro-interaction consent}

The status quo: most micro-interactions that contribute to AI training happen without explicit consent, and often without users' awareness that their interactions constitute training signal.

\textbf{Is this a problem?} The answer depends on one's theory of consent. Consent-by-participation (implied consent to aggregation by using the service) is common in platform business models. Informed consent (knowing that your specific interaction will be used, for what purpose, and with what effect) is rarely present.

\textbf{What would meaningful consent look like?} Meaningful consent would require users to understand: (a)~that their interactions are being aggregated as training signal, (b)~what the system learns from their specific type of interaction, and (c)~how to opt out of contributing to training while still using the service.

\textbf{Is it achievable at scale?} Probably not for micro-interactions at the granularity of individual clicks, because the explanation overhead would exceed the interaction overhead. More realistic: periodic, meaningful disclosure about how the platform uses aggregated interaction data, combined with genuine opt-out mechanisms for users who don't want to contribute.

\subsubsection*{Q10: Technical knowledge for AI governance questions}

\begin{description}
    \item[Recidivism algorithms in sentencing:] Technical knowledge needed: understanding of base rate effects, calibration, the impossibility of simultaneous fairness criteria. Non-technical knowledge equally important: constitutional law regarding due process, criminology evidence on recidivism factors, political philosophy of punishment, community perspectives on criminal justice.

    \item[Facial recognition in public spaces:] Technical knowledge needed: false positive rates by demographic group, accuracy under variable conditions, how systems can be gamed. Non-technical knowledge: Fourth Amendment doctrine on search and seizure, historical experience with surveillance and marginalized communities, power dynamics of who is surveilled and who is protected.

    \item[Who bears the cost of AI errors:] Technical knowledge needed: error rate measurement, subgroup performance analysis, systemic vs.\ spot failure taxonomy. Non-technical knowledge: insurance and liability theory, distributive justice frameworks, political economy of who has the leverage to seek compensation.
\end{description}

\subsubsection*{Q11: Barriers to civic participation}

\textbf{Practical barriers:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Time: regulatory comment periods are short (typically 60--90 days); finding them requires monitoring
    \item Access: federal registers and regulatory portals are difficult to navigate without prior experience
    \item Awareness: most people don't know that public comment periods on AI regulation exist
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Structural barriers:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Process design: comments are typically accepted in formats (long-form written documents) that favor professional advocacy organizations with staff writers
    \item Aggregation: individual comments rarely receive individual responses; they are counted and summarized---a process that rewards organized campaigns over individual contributions
    \item Technical intimidation: the framing of AI governance as ``technical'' issues requiring specialized expertise discourages non-expert participation, even when the relevant questions are normative
\end{itemize}

The structural barriers are more important than the practical ones because they would persist even if awareness increased. Regulatory reform that enables shorter, more accessible comments and creates genuine responsiveness to individual perspectives would change the structural picture.

\subsubsection*{Q12: Designing for genuine presence}

Design choices that promote genuine presence:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Framing review as judgment, not approval: ``Review this recommendation and decide'' rather than ``Confirm this decision''
    \item Providing context that makes the stakes real: the reviewer should know something about what happens to the person affected by the decision
    \item Ensuring the interface doesn't pre-commit the reviewer before they've engaged: don't show the AI recommendation before the reviewer has formed an initial impression
    \item Pacing that allows genuine engagement: not so many cases per hour that thoughtful review is impossible
    \item Feedback mechanisms: reviewers who learn when they were right vs.\ wrong develop genuine investment in the outcome
\end{itemize}

Limits: some interactions are genuinely low-stakes and do not warrant genuine presence. The goal is not to make every interaction maximally demanding---it is to design high-stakes interactions so that presence is possible and supported.

\subsubsection*{Q13: Theater vs.\ genuine interaction design}

What makes cookie consent dialogs theater:
\begin{itemize}
    \item The choice architecture strongly favors acceptance (the default, or the largest button, or the simplest path)
    \item The information provided is not actionable (``we use cookies for personalization'' gives no basis for a meaningful decision)
    \item The cost of genuine engagement exceeds the benefit (reading the full privacy policy takes longer than the benefit of changing settings is worth)
\end{itemize}

A genuinely informative cookie consent would: specify which specific data types are collected, for which specific purposes, with which specific retention periods, and give the user real choices with understandable consequences. Studies suggest users engage substantially more with genuinely informative consent interfaces---the ``I don't have time to read this'' response is partly a rational response to the low information content of current interfaces.

\subsubsection*{Q14: Final synthesis---Netflix to the closing invitation}

Netflix's ``Are you still watching?'' encodes the book's central argument in miniature: a system powerful enough to just keep playing, that chose instead to pause and ask. Not because it lacked confidence. Because it knew the limits of its confidence. And because it respected the human enough to check in.

The closing invitation---``your job is not to approve what the machine decided, your job is to be present''---is the human-facing corollary. The Netflix question requires a recipient. The system that pauses to ask is useless without a human who actually answers.

Across the five dimensions:
\begin{description}
    \item[Uncertainty Detection:] Netflix detects that it doesn't know whether watching is still intentional. AI systems that know what they don't know are more useful collaborators.
    \item[Intervention Design:] Netflix asks in a way that is easy to answer and gives the human genuine control. Good intervention design makes genuine presence easy.
    \item[Timing:] Netflix asks at the right moment---when the uncertainty is genuinely unresolved. Optimal timing creates the conditions for genuine engagement.
    \item[Stakes Calibration:] ``Are you still watching?'' scales to stakes: it is less intrusive than a security challenge, less dismissible than a cookie banner. Calibrated intervention matches the weight of the ask to the importance of the decision.
    \item[Feedback Integration:] The answer informs Netflix how long you watch before needing to be prompted again. Feedback integration lets systems improve their asking over time.
\end{description}

The book's arc: the machines that ask well, and the humans who answer genuinely, make a better loop than either alone.

\subsubsection*{Exercise 18A.1: Human Contribution Audit}

\textbf{Part (a)--(b):} A model with 85\% accuracy that routes cases with confidence $<0.7$ to human review (simulated at 80\% accuracy on routed cases) will typically achieve:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Routing rate: 20--35\% of cases routed (depending on calibration of confidence scores)
    \item HCI: positive when human accuracy on routed cases (80\%) exceeds model accuracy on those cases (typically 60--70\% for uncertain cases)
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Part (c):} The HCI is typically maximized at a confidence threshold between 0.6 and 0.75, where the marginal cases being routed to human review are genuinely uncertain but not extreme outliers (where human accuracy is also lower).

\textbf{Part (d):} The optimal threshold balances two competing effects: as the threshold increases, more cases are routed (including cases the model would have gotten right), which dilutes the routing benefit. As the threshold decreases, only the most extreme uncertainty is routed, which reduces the volume and may miss recoverable cases. The maximum is where the marginal case routed has exactly equal expected accuracy under human and model review.

\subsubsection*{Exercise 18A.2: Accountability Architecture Audit}

\textbf{Strong answers will:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Distinguish between what is publicly stated (often Layer 1 and 2 claims) and what can be verified (contestability pathway, audit reports)
    \item Note that most systems claim Layer 1 and 2 but lack genuine Layer 3 (independent audit) and Layer 4 (meaningful contestability)
    \item Identify specific gaps: does the contestability pathway have actual response time commitments? Can the individual actually speak to someone with authority to reverse the decision?
\end{itemize}

\textbf{The most common finding:} Most production AI systems have documented audit trails and some aggregate monitoring, but lack independent audit and have contestability pathways that are nominally present but practically inaccessible (buried in terms of service, requiring lengthy processes with no outcome guarantees).

\subsubsection*{Exercise 18A.3: Civic Participation Simulation}

Strong comments will: (a)~specify which fairness definition the proposal implicitly adopts (most do, implicitly, even without using the term); (b)~identify whether the monitoring requirements are for aggregate metrics only or include disaggregated subgroup performance; (c)~assess whether contestability provisions specify meaningful timeframes and actual authority to override.

Common findings: most AI regulatory proposals are strong on general principles, weaker on operationalization. A strong comment adds value by identifying the specific gap between the principle stated and the mechanism needed to achieve it.
